NGO and Donor World

by Admin | Category big data | Sept 16th, 2021

0 Comments

I had previously talked about how not-for-profit organizations can best raise funds to implement their projects. However, some may still be asking questions concerning donors, presenting the need to demystify some myths that can help NGOs understand and build a great working relationship with donors. These are some of the important information that NGOs need to have:

1. Donors are Experts

Majority of donors are experts especially recruited from not-for-profit organizations and development agencies. When such donors receive proposals, they are eager to review the programs’ impact and delve into the technical components of the program instead of dwelling on the budgetary issues. NGOs seeking for funding must therefore ensure that their proposals and program details are professionally reviewed to meet all the acceptable standards including sustainability. This happens best when NGOs consult a donor concerning the program which also helps in strengthening the relationship.

2. Keep Trying

When your program’s proposal is not accepted by a donor, it doesn’t mean that the donor doesn’t like your program. Often, donors may have run out of budgets or time-frame for funding. The donor may also not be interested in your geographical location since funding is sometimes done with this is consideration. In many cases, ‘NO’ means ‘TRY LATER’. Under such circumstances, NGOs need to ask for feedback to establish why their proposals never resulted in funding and use this as an opportunity to develop relationship with the donor.

3. Donors Can’t Do Without You

Donors are not implementing agencies or entities but they typically have identified specific problems in the world that they must solve. This is why donors will lobby for resources and give them to NGOs to implement projects they are aligned to. This is how donors achieve their objectives of making the world a better place. It is therefore requisite for NGOs to realize that the resources granted to them must be managed properly because they are not mere recipients of such resources. Not for profit organizations must also look at how best they can help the donors achieve their goals by linking donor objectives with their own in order for both parties to succeed in their charitable missions. The type of relationship between NGOs and donors is therefore built on strong partnership meaning, that NGOs should offer real solutions that create change.

4. Some Donors Don’t Like Risks

Some donors love working with programs that have been tested and are proven to produce desirable results. However, others love supporting highly innovative but untested programs that seem to bear high rewards. Having said this, it is important for NGOs to note that all programs that have real impact whether new or old get funding from donors. This means that however young your program may seem, it will get resources for implementation from donors if it can create impact in the society.

5. Donors Love Negotiated Deals

Donors love programs that have been optimized to offer greatest impact with arguably moderate funds. This implies that programs should not be very expensive but should be designed to create impact that is higher than the resources put in implementing the program. Often, the scale of work expected or stipulated by the donors may not suit the operational technicalities of an NGO. In such cases, NGOs must negotiate equitable conditions for both before signing a contract with the donor.

6. Donors Like Invitations

Donors like meeting professionals who are helping them achieve their goals on the ground when invited. It is therefore a good gesture to extend an invitation to donors to visit your program. This helps them experience more opportunities in new countries and gain more knowledge about your program. It also helps in strengthening funding relationship.

7. Surprises are not Good

Not for profit organizations should warn the donor as soon as possible if certain conditions cannot not be achieved within the set time-frame. For instance, reporting deadlines may not be realistic since most of the spending may be taking place in the field, where there may be no internet access to ease communication. It is important to explain this to donors in advance and not wait until you’re late to justify it. Similarly, if certain budget items cost more than budgeted due to unforeseen changes, NGOs should give the donors advance notice in order to foster accountability.
